1. Context & architectural decisions
1.1 The three-stage cycle
OLOS organises all land stewardship work around three stages: Plan, Act, and Observe.

[TABLE]
Stage | Role
Plan | Determines what needs to be known and done. The entry point for any new project. Sequences objectives by dependency, generates Act tasks, and gates downstream decisions behind genuine prerequisite completion.
Act | Executes all field work — both survey/observation tasks and physical implementation tasks. These feel identical in the UI. Completed Act tasks feed data into Observe.
Observe | A read-only intelligence layer. Surfaces synthesised data produced by Act tasks. The user never 'does' Observe — they read it. It grows richer as Act tasks complete.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Key architectural principle / Observe is not the entry point. Plan is. The cycle is: Plan (decide what to learn or do) → Act (execute it) → Observe (read what was learned). A new project always begins in Plan.
[/TABLE]

1.2 Objectives as the primary navigation unit
The previous navigation model used Domain as the entry point (Stage → Domain → Objective). This has been replaced.
The problem with domain-first navigation:
Objectives routinely cross domain boundaries. “Map water movement” requires Topography, Hydrology, Soil, and Climate data simultaneously. Forcing it into a single domain produced incomplete decisions.
Free domain selection allowed users to plan in the wrong order — e.g. designing planting systems before water strategy was resolved — producing expensive rework.
Domain was an organisational lens for data, not a unit of work. Objectives are the actual unit of work.
The new model:
Stage → Objective (domain overlays load automatically based on what the objective requires)
The user never selects a domain. They select what they are trying to understand or decide.
Each objective carries its required overlays, prerequisite objectives, parallelisable peers, and completion gate as first-class data.
1.3 Dependency-gated sequencing
Regenerative land design has hard dependencies that, if violated, produce rework. The Plan stage enforces these as a tier-based dependency graph. A user cannot access Tier N until Tier N-1 is complete (with parallelism within tiers where applicable).
The dependency graph is not paternalistic — it reflects real ecological and design logic:
Water movement cannot be correctly read without terrain data.
Zone allocation requires terrain, water, sectors, and access observations.
Planting system design without resolved water strategy produces redesign.
Locked objectives are informative, not punitive. Clicking a locked tier reveals a plain-language explanation of which prerequisite must complete first.
2. Plan stage tier structure
The Plan stage is organised into seven tiers. Each tier groups objectives by dependency level. Tiers unlock sequentially; objectives within a tier can run in parallel unless noted.
Tier 0 — Project Foundation
No prerequisites. Entry point for all new projects. Must be complete before any land reading begins.

[TABLE]
Objective | Purpose
Define vision, goals & stewardship capacity | Establishes what the steward is trying to achieve, on what timeline, and with what resources. Records hard constraints: labour, budget, skills, access.
Establish site boundaries & legal constraints | Sets the spatial anchor for all future overlays. Records title, easements, zoning, setbacks, watercourse buffers, and protected areas.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Unlocks / Completion of both Tier 0 objectives unlocks Tier 1 — Land Reading.
[/TABLE]

Tier 1 — Land Reading
Requires: Tier 0 complete. All four objectives are parallelisable — any order.

[TABLE]
Objective | Unlocks
Survey terrain & topography | Tier 2: Water Movement Survey, Tier 2: Soil Survey
Survey climate & sectors | Tier 3: Zone Allocation
Survey existing ecology & habitat | Tier 4: Ecology & Guild Design, Tier 5: Animal Integration
Survey existing infrastructure & access | Tier 4: Access & Circulation Design, Tier 4: Infrastructure Design
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Parallelism note / All four Tier 1 surveys can run simultaneously. A steward can begin ecology and infrastructure surveys before terrain is complete. However, Tier 2 will not unlock until Terrain Survey specifically is complete, regardless of other Tier 1 progress.
[/TABLE]

Tier 2 — Systems Reading
Requires: Terrain Survey (Tier 1) complete. Water and soil cannot be correctly read without slope and drainage data.

[TABLE]
Objective | Unlocks
Survey water movement & hydrology | Tier 3: Water Strategy
Survey soil conditions & subsurface | Tier 4: Soil Improvement Design, Tier 4: Planting Systems Design
[/TABLE]

Tier 3 — Foundation Decisions
Requires: All of Tier 1 and Tier 2 complete. These are the load-bearing decisions of the Plan stage — zone framework and water strategy constrain almost everything downstream. The name is cycle-neutral by design: in every subsequent Plan cycle these remain the foundational decisions to revisit and revise, not merely the ‘first’ ones made.

[TABLE]
Objective | Depends on
Establish zone framework | Terrain, water, sectors, access, ecology surveys
Define water strategy | Water movement survey + terrain + zone framework draft
[/TABLE]

Tier 4 — System Design
Requires: Tier 3 complete. Parallelisable within tier with soft intra-tier dependencies noted.

[TABLE]
Objective | Soft dependency within tier
Design access & circulation | Zones + water strategy
Design soil improvement programme | Soil survey + zones
Design planting systems | Zones + water + soil (benefits from access draft)
Design built infrastructure | Zones + water + access
[/TABLE]

Tier 5 — Integration Design
Requires: All Tier 4 approved.

[TABLE]
Objective | Depends on
Design animal integration | Zones + water + planting + ecology
Design energy & resource flows | Infrastructure + zones
Design people, roles & governance | Zones + all Tier 4 designs
[/TABLE]

Tier 6 — Phasing & Resourcing
Requires: All Tier 5 approved. Only possible once all designs are resolved.

[TABLE]
Objective | Purpose
Phase & sequence implementation | Order all Act work packages by ecological and operational dependency across a multi-year timeline.
Budget & resource planning | Assign costs, labour, and materials to each phase. Project-level tracking only — not capital formation (see covenant boundary, section 5).
[/TABLE]

2.8 The Plan–Act–Observe spiral & two-mode gate logic
2.8.1 The cycle is a spiral, not a sequence
Plan → Act → Observe is not a one-time linear sequence. It is a recurring spiral. Act execution changes the land. Observe surfaces what changed. Plan must then revisit prior decisions in light of new evidence and adapt.
This has a direct consequence for the tier structure: the gate logic that governs Tier access must behave differently depending on whether the user is in their initial pass or a subsequent cycle.
2.8.2 Two gate modes

[TABLE]
Mode | When it applies | Gate behaviour
Initial completion | First time through the Plan stage on a new project | Hard gates. A tier is inaccessible until all prerequisite objectives are genuinely complete. The user cannot skip or bypass.
Cyclical review | Any subsequent Plan cycle after Act has executed and Observe has updated | Soft gates. Completed tiers become review checkpoints rather than locked barriers. The user can open any previously completed tier to reassess and revise decisions in light of new Observe data.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Why this matters / In year 3 of a project, a steward should not be forced to re-complete a Terrain Survey before they can revise their Water Strategy. The terrain is known. What has changed is what Observe has surfaced since the last cycle. The gate should ask: has anything changed that affects this tier? — not: have you completed all prerequisites from scratch?
[/TABLE]

2.8.3 UX expression of review mode
In cyclical review mode, the tier spine communicates the shift:

[TABLE]
Tier state | Initial cycle | Cyclical review
Previously completed tier | Complete (green check) | Review (amber refresh icon) — signals that new Observe data may affect this tier’s decisions
Active tier | Blue dot | Blue dot (unchanged)
Locked tier | Lock icon + hard gate | No locked tiers in review mode — all previously completed tiers are accessible
Tier not yet reached | Locked as normal | Locked as normal — tiers the user has never completed remain hard-gated
[/TABLE]

When a user opens a tier in review mode, the objective cards show their last-completed state with a “Review” callout noting the Observe data that has changed since the last cycle. The user can update decisions and re-approve objectives without re-completing the full checklist from scratch.
2.8.4 Cyclical review UI — entry and confirmation screens
The two-mode gate logic described in sections 2.8.1–2.8.3 requires two distinct UI screens to make the review flow legible to stewards.
Screen 1 — Review entry state
When Observe data has updated enough to trigger a Plan revision signal, and the steward enters a tier in cyclical review mode, a modal or banner appears before the tier’s objectives are shown. It explains in plain language what changed and what may be affected.

[TABLE]
Element | Specification
What Observe detected | One or two sentences stating the specific data change. Example: “Soil pH at Zone 2 has dropped from 6.4 to 5.8 since your last review.” Not a generic alert — always specific to the actual data.
Which objectives are flagged | A compact list of the affected objectives with their tier and a one-line reason each is flagged. Each item is tappable.
Primary CTA | ‘Begin Review’ — blue filled button. Opens the tier with all flagged objectives highlighted in amber.
Secondary CTA | ‘Dismiss for now’ — text link. Closes the modal and opens the tier in normal review mode without amber highlights. The Plan revision banner remains visible in Observe.
[/TABLE]

Screen 2 — Review confirmation state
After a steward reviews a flagged objective and either confirms the existing decision or updates it, the right detail panel updates to reflect the outcome.

[TABLE]
Element | Specification
Review badge | The amber ‘Review’ badge on the objective card is replaced with either: (a) a green ‘Decision confirmed’ badge if the steward confirmed the existing decision, or (b) a blue ‘Decision updated’ badge if they made a change.
Confirmation summary | A compact one-line summary in the detail panel below the objective title: ‘Confirmed on [date]: [decision summary]’ or ‘Updated on [date]: [what changed]’.
Downstream effects | If the decision update affects downstream objectives, a compact list of affected objectives appears below the summary with a ‘Review now’ link each.
Plan revision banner | Once all flagged objectives in the tier are reviewed, the Plan revision banner in Observe clears for this tier.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Review mode feels different from first-cycle mode / In cyclical review, the steward is not completing things for the first time. They are assessing whether prior decisions still hold. The UI must communicate this difference — amber review badges, confirmation language, and the Observe data context panel all signal that this is a revision session, not initial planning.
[/TABLE]

2.8.4 Tier 3 naming rationale
Tier 3 is named “Foundation Decisions” rather than “First Design Decisions” precisely because of the spiral. The zone framework and water strategy are the foundational decisions in every cycle — they are the ones everything else depends on whether you are making them for the first time or revising them in year 5. The word “first” would become incorrect and confusing on every cycle after the initial one.
3. UI architecture
3.1 Three-panel shell
The Plan Command Center uses a fixed three-panel layout. All panels are always visible on desktop. Panels do not collapse or stack at desktop viewport widths.

[TABLE]
Panel | Role
Left — Tier Spine (210px) | Shows all seven tiers with status, progress count, and sequential position. Primary project orientation surface.
Centre — Objective Cards (flexible) | Shows the objectives within the selected tier. The 'next up' affordance lives here. Cards are the selection surface.
Right — Objective Detail (300px) | Shows the active objective's checklist, map activation strip, and supporting context. The primary work surface.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Mobile / Act note / This three-panel layout is appropriate for the Plan stage (primary action: deciding) on desktop and tablet. The Act Command Center requires a fundamentally different treatment: fewer panels, larger touch targets, camera and GPS front and centre. Do not apply this shell to Act.
[/TABLE]

3.2 Progress bar
A full-width bar spans all three panels at the top of the shell. It provides project-level orientation above the tier-level detail of the spine.

[TABLE]
Element | Behaviour
Stage pills (Plan / Act / Observe) | Indicates current stage. Non-active stages are clickable navigation targets to their respective command centers.
Progress label | Static label: “Project progress”
Progress track | Fills proportionally to decisions made across all tiers. Updates live as checklist items are completed and objectives are gated. Label reads: ‘Design Decisions: N of N made’
Percentage readout | Rounds to nearest integer. Updates live.
Objectives counter | Format: “N / N objectives complete”. Updates live.
[/TABLE]

3.3 Tier spine (left panel)
The spine is the project’s sequencing backbone. It must always show the user where they are in the full Plan stage at a glance.
Tier states

[TABLE]
State | Visual treatment
Complete | Green dot with check icon. Green connector line to next tier.
Active | Blue dot with tier number. Blue left border on the row. Blue text.
Available | Gray dot with tier number. No border. Muted text.
Locked | Gray dot with lock icon. Muted text. Cursor: pointer (not blocked).
[/TABLE]

Locked tier interaction — popover + bypass
Clicking a locked tier opens a small popover anchored to the tier row — not a full modal. The popover is informative, not punitive. No red colours or warning iconography. Use the existing amber/gold palette throughout.

[TABLE]
Popover element | Specification
Lock reason | Plain language. Example: “Requires: Water Movement Survey + Soil Survey complete”
Progress indicator | Shows how many prerequisites are met. Example: “1 of 2 requirements met” with a small progress bar or pip row in amber.
Bypass link | A subtle secondary link at the bottom of the popover in small muted text: “I understand the dependencies — open anyway”. Not a button — a text link, intentionally low affordance.
[/TABLE]

Bypass confirmation state
If the user clicks “Open anyway”, the popover transitions to a minimal acknowledgment prompt:
Text: “You’re entering this tier out of sequence. Some decisions may lack the data they need. Continue?”
Two actions: Confirm (amber filled button) and Cancel (text link). No destructive red styling.
On Confirm: the tier opens in a visually distinct ‘out of sequence’ state — a subtle amber left border on the tier row and an amber banner at the top of the centre panel reading: “You’re reviewing this tier out of sequence. Decisions made here may need revisiting once prerequisites are complete.”
The banner is dismissible. It does not prevent any action.

[TABLE]
Tone principle / The bypass exists because experienced stewards sometimes need to explore downstream tiers before prerequisites are technically complete — for scoping, estimation, or familiarity. The system must not punish this. The amber out-of-sequence state is a reminder, not a gate.
[/TABLE]

3.4 Objective cards (centre panel)
Next up affordance
The first element in the centre panel (above the card list) is the “Next up” card. This is the most important single element in the entire Plan navigation shell.
If an objective is in progress (status: active), the card shows: “Continue where you left off” with the objective title and a count of completed checklist steps.
If no objective is in progress but one is available (status: available), the card shows: “Start here” with the objective title.
Clicking the card selects the objective and loads its detail in the right panel.
This card answers the question: “What do I do right now?” without requiring the user to scan the full card list.

[TABLE]
Project home screen note / The “Next up” logic must also exist at the project home screen level — above the Plan Command Center entirely. A returning user who has been in the field for two weeks should see their next action on the home screen without having to navigate into Plan first.
[/TABLE]

Parallel callout
When a tier contains parallelisable objectives, a callout card appears above the objective list (below the Next Up card if present). It states:
Primary line: “These can be done in any order”
Secondary line: The specific condition that must be met before the next tier unlocks (e.g. “All four needed before Tier 2 unlocks”)
This callout replaces the generic group label that would otherwise appear. It answers the anxiety of “Am I doing this wrong?” before it arises.
Card states

[TABLE]
Status | Badge label | Badge colour
complete | Complete | Green
active | Active inquiry | Blue
available | Ready to enter | Amber
locked | Not yet reached | Gray (muted)
[/TABLE]

Ready to enter must be visually distinct from Not yet reached. Amber/yellow communicates availability. Gray communicates inaccessibility.
Card metadata
Each objective card shows three metadata chips below the description:
Checklist step count (e.g. “4 steps”)
Field action count (e.g. “3 field actions”)
Overlay count (e.g. “4 overlays”)
If the objective unlocks downstream objectives, an inline unlock row appears at the bottom of the card: “Unlocks: [objective name] · [objective name]”
3.4b Tier unlock transition state
When the final prerequisite objective in a tier completes and the next tier unlocks, the centre panel displays a full-width unlock transition state before returning to the normal objective card view. This is the primary moment of felt progress in the system.

[TABLE]
Element | Specification
Celebration banner | Full-width card across the centre panel. Displays: tier number, tier name (e.g. ‘Tier 1 Unlocked — Land Reading’), and a one-sentence description of what this tier means for the project. Background: subtle blue fill. Not a toast — a full-width persistent card until dismissed or acted on.
Tier spine update | Tier 0 transitions to the Complete state (green check). Tier 1 transitions from Locked to Active state (blue circle). The lock icon animates away — a 300ms fade and scale-down. The new active circle fades in.
What just became possible | Below the banner, a compact section lists 2–3 downstream objectives that are now accessible. Label: ‘What just became possible’. Each item shows objective title and a brief one-line description. Tapping an item selects it and loads its detail panel.
Dismiss behaviour | The banner auto-dismisses after 8 seconds or on any user interaction (clicking an objective card, advancing to the new tier, etc.). It does not block interaction.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
This moment earns the system trust / A steward who completes two surveys and sees the system respond with ‘Tier 1 Unlocked’ and a clear picture of what just became possible has experienced the dependency logic as a reward, not a constraint. This transition is small in code but significant in felt experience.
[/TABLE]

3.5 Objective detail (right panel)
The detail panel is the primary work surface. It is organised into four explicit sections separated by subtle dividers. The sections are visually distinct so a new user immediately knows to start at section 3 (Your Decisions) and work downward. The section structure is:

[TABLE]
Section | Content | Default state
1. OBJECTIVE | Title and planning question. Unchanged from current spec. Stays prominent. | Always visible
2. MAP ACTIVATION | Overlay pill row with green pulse dot and overlay count. Unchanged from current spec. | Always visible
3. YOUR DECISIONS | The decision list with feeds-into consequence tags. Primary content. Highest visual weight. | Always visible and expanded
4. REFERENCE | Field actions, Feeds Observe, Completion gate, Unlocks. Supporting context only. | Collapsed by default. Expandable via ‘Reference’ toggle.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
Section 3 is the entry point / A new user scanning the detail panel for the first time should land on ‘Your Decisions’ automatically. Sections 1 and 2 are orientation; Section 3 is action; Section 4 is reference. The visual weight and vertical position must reinforce this reading order.
[/TABLE]

Header (fixed)

[TABLE]
Element | Content
Eyebrow | “Active objective” — static label
Title | Objective title
Planning question | The core question this objective answers. Displayed as italic secondary text.
[/TABLE]

Map activation strip (fixed, below header)
Sits between the header and the scrollable body. Makes explicit that selecting an objective is a map action, not just a task selection.
Green pulse dot
Text: “N overlays will activate on the map”
First two overlay names shown as chips, with a +N chip if more than two

[TABLE]
Why this element matters / Without the map activation strip, the detail panel reads as a standalone task manager disconnected from the map. The strip makes the panel feel like a map control — which is what it is. This is one of the most load-bearing UX decisions in the spec.
[/TABLE]

Your Decisions (scrollable body, primary)
The decision list is the primary content of the detail panel. It must look and feel like the most important thing on the screen when the panel is open. The section is labelled ‘Your Decisions’ — not ‘Checklist’ or ‘Steps’. This is a thinking and deciding process, not a task completion process.
Section label: “Your Decisions” with a right-aligned count (“N / N decisions made”)
Checkboxes are 15×15px with clear hit targets
Unchecked item text: primary text colour, full weight
Checked item text: tertiary text colour, strikethrough
Checking an item updates the progress bar and done count live

[TABLE]
Hierarchy rule / Your Decisions is what the user does right now. All other content in the detail panel is supporting context. The decision list must have higher visual weight than everything below it. Labels reinforce that this is a methodology — not a to-do list.
[/TABLE]

Details toggle (scrollable body, secondary)
Below the checklist, a collapsible “Details” toggle reveals supporting context. It is collapsed by default.
The toggle label is “Details” — not “Context & dependencies” or any other abstract phrase. The user should not have to interpret what is inside.
Contents when expanded:
Field actions — the field tasks this objective generates, shown as a list with a shovel icon
Feeds Observe — a description of what Observe data this objective’s Act tasks will populate
Completion gate — the specific criteria that mark this objective as done
Completing this unlocks — shown only when the objective has downstream dependencies
Footer (fixed)
A single outlined button: “Launch Act Command Center”.
Outlined (not filled) — it is a navigation action, not the primary CTA for the screen
Pinned to the bottom of the panel so it is always reachable without scrolling
Passes the active objective’s context to the Act Command Center on click
Label is “Launch Act Command Center” — not “Generate Act Tasks” (tasks are already shown in the Details section)
4. Objective data model
Each objective in the Plan stage carries the following fields as first-class data. Domain is a tag on the objective, not a navigation gate.

[TABLE]
Field | Type and description
id | string — unique identifier
tier_id | integer — which tier this objective belongs to (0–6)
title | string — short display name
planning_question | string — the core question this objective answers
description | string — one-sentence description for the card view
status | enum: complete | active | available | locked
domain_ids[] | string[] — which domains this objective draws on (plural, not singular)
overlay_ids[] | string[] — map overlays that activate when this objective is selected
prerequisite_objective_ids[] | string[] — objectives that must be complete before this unlocks
parallel_with_ids[] | string[] — objectives in the same tier that can run concurrently
checklist_items[] | ChecklistItem[] — ordered decisions with completion state. Each item carries: text, is_methodology boolean, and feeds_into string (the downstream consequence displayed as muted helper text).
act_task_ids[] | string[] — Act tasks generated by this objective
observe_feeds | string — description of what Observe data this objective produces
completion_gate | string — criteria that mark this objective complete
unlocks_objective_ids[] | string[] — objectives that unlock when this completes
[/TABLE]

4.1 Status resolution rules
Objective status is computed, not manually set. The resolution order is:
If all checklist items are done and the completion gate is met: status = complete
Else if any checklist item is done: status = active
Else if all prerequisite_objective_ids are complete: status = available
Else: status = locked
5. Stage boundary rules & covenant constraints
5.1 What belongs in Plan
Plan objectives are decision-making surfaces. Allowed verbs: decide, design, compare, select, reject, prioritise, sequence, phase, estimate, approve, define, specify, constrain, resolve, defer.
Plan must not:
Collect raw baseline observations without reference to a decision
Assign workers to field execution
Mark Act tasks complete
Perform construction or physical implementation
Replace legal, engineering, ecological, or professional review when required
5.2 Plan outputs
When a Plan objective reaches a decision, it produces one of the following statuses:
Approved for Act
Conditionally Approved (with named conditions)
Needs More Observation (flags a gap in Observe data)
Needs Professional Review
Redesign Required
Deferred
Rejected
Act should only receive Approved or Conditionally Approved handoff packages.
5.3 Covenant boundary — Plan vs Economics

[TABLE]
Covenant constraint / Tier 6 (Phasing & Resourcing) covers project cost and budget tracking only. Capital formation, financing, advance-purchase agreements, investor or equity structures, and yield-as-return framing are explicitly excluded from Plan and fall under Sub-project C (Economics), which is governed by Scholar Council review. No riba or gharar framing in any Plan surface.
[/TABLE]

6. Developer acceptance criteria
6.1 Navigation
A new project enters at Tier 0. No other tier is accessible until Tier 0 is complete.
Clicking a locked tier reveals an inline callout explaining the prerequisite. Clicking again dismisses it. The tier row is never blocked or inert.
Clicking an unlocked tier switches the centre panel to that tier’s objectives without page reload.
Selecting an objective populates the detail panel without navigating away from the Command Center.
6.2 Next up affordance
The Next Up card always reflects the current project state — it is not static.
If an active objective exists, Next Up shows “Continue where you left off” with correct step count.
If no active objective but an available one exists, Next Up shows “Start here.”
If all objectives in the current tier are complete, Next Up does not show (the tier is complete; user should advance to the next tier).
6.3 Progress bar
Progress percentage and objective count update immediately on checklist item interaction — no page reload.
Progress reflects completed objectives across all tiers, not just the active tier.
Stage pills correctly indicate current stage and navigate to other stage command centers on click.
6.4 Detail panel
Map activation strip shows the correct overlay count and first two overlay names for the selected objective.
Checklist items persist state within the session. Checked items show strikethrough.
Done count in the checklist label updates live.
Details toggle is collapsed by default. State persists within the session (if user expanded it, it stays expanded when they return to the same objective).
Launch Act Command Center button passes the active objective ID and title to the Act Command Center.
6.5 Objective status
Status is computed from checklist state and prerequisite completion — it is never manually set.
Completing the final checklist item of an objective does not automatically mark it complete — the completion gate must also be met.
When an objective completes, dependent objectives in downstream tiers update their lock state immediately.
6b. Visual language — land methodology aesthetic
The visual design of the Plan Command Center should feel native to land and methodology work — not generic SaaS. The following visual language shifts are specified for implementation. Layout and information architecture remain unchanged.

[TABLE]
Element | Specification
Active tier circle | Replace the flat blue filled circle with a circle that has a slightly irregular, organic edge — achieved via an SVG path or border-radius variation rather than a perfect geometric circle. The effect is subtle: land, not software.
Decision checkboxes | Replace square checkboxes with circular marks. An unchecked item shows an empty circle outline. A checked item shows a filled circle with a checkmark. This small change shifts the component from task-list to methodology-marker.
Centre panel background | Apply a very subtle topographic line texture at 5% opacity to the centre panel background. The texture is present but not dominant — visible on close inspection, not attention-seeking. SVG pattern or CSS background-image.
Objective title typography | Objective titles in the detail panel header and objective cards use a serif typeface (e.g. the Lora typeface established in earlier OLOS design work). UI chrome (labels, counts, badges, navigation) remains in the system sans-serif. The distinction signals: this content is methodology, not interface.
Tier unlock celebration banner | Uses the same organic circle motif from the active tier — the newly unlocked tier number rendered large in the organic circle, centre-aligned in the banner.
[/TABLE]


[TABLE]
The goal is felt difference, not visual noise / Every visual language change should pass this test: does it make the product feel more like it was designed for land? If the answer is no, or if the change adds visual noise rather than meaning, do not implement it. Subtle and intentional outperforms decorative every time.
[/TABLE]
