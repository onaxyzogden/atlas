**1. Is "cross-section + solar overlay" the right framing for a permaculture Module 6, or should the orthodox framing be different?**

The **"cross-section + solar overlay" framing used in Option A (Atlas) is highly orthodox and essential** to the permaculture design process. In the Oregon State University Permaculture Design Course (OSU PDC), the "Site Cross Section" is a dedicated, major assignment (Assignment 15) that requires designers to draw a transect line (typically labeled A to A' or A to B) across the site's plan view and illustrate the vertical profile of the landscape [1, 2]. 

Crucially, **the inclusion of summer and winter solar angles is a mandatory requirement for these cross-sections.** The curriculum explicitly instructs students to "Include the SUMMER and WINTER solar angle to consider the solar aspect of your design" [3]. By visualizing the solstice altitudes, designers can determine how the height of trees and structures will cast shadows or allow solar access throughout the seasons [4-6].

While Sector Analysis (wind, fire, sun paths) and Keyline design (slope-water-tree integration) are also critical permaculture frameworks, they are treated as separate analytical layers. Sector analysis is typically mapped top-down on a 360-degree "Sector Compass" (Assignment 7) to show directional forces coming into the site from the outside [7-9]. The cross-section, however, is where all these elements—water, slope, trees, and structures—are integrated vertically to understand their physical and spatial relationships [10-12]. Therefore, Option B (OGDEN), which restricts the cross-section to just a swale long-profile, is far too limited. A proper permaculture cross-section is meant to capture "many elements," "elevation change," and "overlap with tenants," making Option A the correct holistic framing [4].

**2. Is the Atlas combination sufficient, or are critical things missing?**

The Atlas combination of freeform pinning, typed references to other stores, and solstice altitude lines provides a remarkably strong foundation, but **it is currently missing a few critical text and bracketing features required for a complete permaculture cross-section.**

What Atlas gets right:
*   **Typed References and Heights:** Your use of typed references that resolve heights (e.g., swales, trees, structures) is a massive asset. The sources note that "Knowing the tree heights clarified solar access and shading patterns shown in the sector compass" [13].
*   **Physical Elements:** The OSU PDC requires cross-sections to show "existing elements - Structures, fences, animals, plants, roads, footpaths" [11]. Atlas's ability to pin structures, trees, shrubs, and water features perfectly serves this need.

What Atlas is missing:
*   **Microclimate Zones:** A fundamental requirement of the permaculture cross-section is identifying and labeling the microclimates along the transect [10, 11, 13]. Designers must be able to bracket sections of the profile and label them with attributes like "Shady, Dry, Warm, Windy" or "Full sun, wet in rainy season, hot, flat" [11, 14]. 
*   **Succession Stages:** Similarly, the cross-section must identify the ecological succession stages across the profile (e.g., "Early Succession - compacted, disturbed," "Mid Succession - grasses, pioneer species," or "Late stage / climax community") [4, 10, 14]. 
*   **Slope/Elevation Data:** While your tool likely maps the visual slope, orthodox cross-sections require explicit notations of the elevation changes and slope percentages (e.g., "Slope 20-25%") [11].
*   **Wind and Flow Deflection:** While wind and fire origin directions belong on the top-down Sector Compass [7, 9], the vertical cross-section is often used to illustrate *how* an element responds to those sectors. For example, showing a dense evergreen tree deflecting a cold winter wind over a structure, or a swale catching overland water flow [15-17].

**3. Verdict: PORT_OGDEN | KEEP_ATLAS | BUILD_FRESH**

**Verdict: KEEP_ATLAS (with minor enhancements)**

**Rationale:** Option A (Atlas) should absolutely be kept as the foundation for Module 6. It already accomplishes the heavy lifting of the OSU PDC "Site Cross Section" assignment by providing the A-B transect, vertical element plotting, and the highly specific winter/summer solstice solar overlay [1, 3]. 

Option B (OGDEN) should be rejected for this module because embedding a cross-section *only* inside a water/swale tool severely misunderstands the broad utility of the cross-section in permaculture. A true cross-section is the ultimate synthesis tool used to verify that trees, water systems, human access, and structures are harmoniously integrated and not negatively shading or competing with one another [4, 18, 19]. 

To make Atlas perfectly "permaculture-sound," you do not need to build fresh; you simply need to **add a feature that allows the steward to draw horizontal brackets beneath the 2D profile to label "Microclimates" and "Succession Stages"** [10, 11, 14]. With that addition, Atlas will be a near-perfect digital reflection of professional permaculture cross-section methodology.
